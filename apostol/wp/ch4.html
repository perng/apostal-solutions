Essential Definitions and Theorems

Definition 1 (Cauchy Sequence) A sequence $latex {(x_n)}&fg=000000$ in a metric space $latex {(S,d)}&fg=000000$ is Cauchy if for every $latex {\varepsilon > 0}&fg=000000$ there exists $latex {N \in \mathbb{N}}&fg=000000$ such that $latex {d(x_n, x_m) < \varepsilon}&fg=000000$ for all $latex {n, m \geq N}&fg=000000$.

\noindentImportance: Cauchy sequences provide a way to test for convergence without knowing the limit in advance. This is crucial in many applications where we can verify that terms are getting closer together but don't know what they're converging to. The concept of completeness (every Cauchy sequence converges) is fundamental to modern analysis.

\noindentImportance: This is one of the most fundamental convergence results in analysis. It provides a simple criterion for when powers of a complex number converge to zero, which is essential for understanding power series, geometric series, and many iterative processes.

Theorem 3 (Ratio Test) Let $latex {(a_n)}&fg=000000$ be a sequence of positive numbers. If $latex {\limsup_{n \rightarrow \infty} \frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n} < 1}&fg=000000$, then $latex {\sum a_n}&fg=000000$ converges. If $latex {\liminf_{n \rightarrow \infty} \frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n} > 1}&fg=000000$, then $latex {\sum a_n}&fg=000000$ diverges.

\noindentImportance: The ratio test is one of the most powerful and practical convergence tests for series. It compares consecutive terms to determine convergence, making it particularly useful for series with factorial or exponential terms. The use of limsup and liminf makes it robust even when the ratio doesn't have a simple limit.
